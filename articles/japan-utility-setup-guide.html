Setting Up Utilities in Japan: A Guide for New Residents

Updated April 11, 2026

Starting Your Home in Japan: Utility Connections

Japan welcomes many new residents each year. Setting up a home here involves connecting essential services. It can seem like a puzzle at first.

Most utility applications require a resident card (在留カード, zairyu card) and a Japanese bank account or a credit card. Some providers accept foreign credit cards, though this varies by provider. Having these ready makes the process smoother.

Timing matters. It is best to apply for electricity, gas, and water a week or two before moving in. Internet and phone contracts often take longer.

Electricity: Powering the Everyday

Electricity in Japan is supplied by regional companies. For example, TEPCO (Tokyo Electric Power Company) covers the Kanto region, while KEPCO (Kansai Electric Power Company) serves Kansai. Chubu Electric Power operates in the Chubu area.

Applications can be made online through their official websites or by phone. Many companies offer English support lines. It helps to check their specific pages for foreign residents.

Most homes now use smart meters. Usage is tracked automatically. Plans typically involve a basic monthly fee plus a charge based on consumption. As of 2024, a basic plan might start around 1,000 yen, with usage adding to that. Rates vary by provider and plan. Always check the latest rates on their websites.

Activation is usually quick. Often, power turns on the same day or the next business day after application. Sometimes, it is already on in a vacant apartment, and one just needs to register details.

Gas: For Cooking and Warmth

Gas services are also regional. Tokyo Gas is the main provider for the Tokyo area, and Osaka Gas for Osaka. Other regions have their own local companies.

Applying for gas is similar to electricity: online or by phone. However, gas requires a specific step. A technician must visit the apartment to open the gas valve and perform a safety check. Someone needs to be home for this appointment.

This appointment is important. It cannot be skipped. Scheduling it can sometimes take a few days, especially during peak moving seasons. It is wise to arrange this well in advance of the move-in date.

Costs are structured with a basic monthly fee and a usage charge. Like electricity, these rates change. Check the provider's official website for current pricing.

Water: The Essential Flow

Water services are managed by local municipal water bureaus. Each city or ward has its own office. For example, the Tokyo Metropolitan Government Bureau of Waterworks handles Tokyo.

Application methods vary. Some allow online registration, others prefer phone calls, or even require forms sent by mail. In some rental properties, the landlord or management company handles the initial setup, and the resident only needs to register their name for billing.

Water bills are typically issued every two months. The cost depends on consumption and the specific city's rates. There is usually a fixed basic charge, then a tiered rate for usage. These rates are publicly available on the respective water bureau websites.

Often, water is already connected when moving into an apartment. The process is more about registering the new occupant's details for billing purposes.

Internet: Connecting to the World

Internet setup often takes the longest. Major fiber optic providers include NTT FLET's (which many other companies like SoftBank Hikari, au Hikari, and Rakuten Hikari use as their backbone), and independent services like NURO Hikari.

Choosing a provider involves considering speed, cost, and contract length. Most fiber optic plans come with a 2-year contract. Cancellation fees apply if ending early.

Installation usually requires a technician visit to run cables and set up equipment. This appointment needs to be scheduled. It can take anywhere from two weeks to two months for fiber optic installation. Mobile Wi-Fi routers offer a quicker alternative, often available within a few days, but speeds can be less consistent.

Monthly fees vary widely, from around 3,000 to 6,000 yen or more, depending on the plan and provider. Installation fees can be substantial, sometimes 20,000 to 40,000 yen, though many providers offer campaigns to waive or discount these.

Mobile Phone: Your Lifeline

Securing a Japanese phone number is often one of the first things a new resident does. Major carriers are Docomo, au, and SoftBank. These offer full services but can be more expensive.

Many prefer MVNOs (Mobile Virtual Network Operators) for cost savings. Rakuten Mobile, Y!mobile, and UQ mobile are popular choices. They use the infrastructure of the major carriers but offer cheaper plans.

A resident card and a Japanese bank account or credit card are essential for signing a contract. Some providers offer SIM-only plans, which are good for those who bring their own unlocked phone. eSIM options are also becoming more common.

Contract lengths vary. Major carriers often have 2-year contracts, while MVNOs sometimes offer more flexible month-to-month options. Costs can range from 1,000 yen for basic MVNO plans to 8,000 yen or more for unlimited data plans from major carriers, as of 2024.

The Setup Process: Common Missteps and a Quiet Insight

Moving to a new country brings its own set of challenges. Some common oversights often surface during utility setup.

One frequent misstep involves the gas connection. Many people expect it to be as simple as electricity, just turning a switch. But the required technician visit, needing someone present, often catches new residents off guard. Scheduling this appointment needs foresight. A missed appointment means no hot water, no cooking. It is a small detail, but it shapes the first few days in a new home.

Another common mistake is underestimating the time for internet installation. The idea of instant connection, common in some places, does not always apply here. Fiber optic installation can stretch for weeks, sometimes months. Relying solely on this for immediate connectivity can lead to frustration. A mobile Wi-Fi router can bridge this gap, offering a temporary solution until the main line is active.

A small insight, perhaps a pro tip, comes from bundling services. Many utility providers now offer combined plans. For instance, an electricity company might also provide gas, or an internet provider might offer a mobile phone plan. These bundles often come with small discounts. It simplifies billing, too, reducing the number of separate payments each month. It is a quiet way to simplify things.

Checking for English support is also a subtle advantage. Most major utility and internet providers have dedicated English hotlines or sections on their websites. It removes a layer of linguistic friction. The process becomes less about translation and more about simply getting things done.

The quiet hum of a refrigerator, the soft glow of a lamp, the gentle rush of water from the tap. These small things make a house a home. Setting them up takes patience. But once they are in place, a sense of quiet calm settles in.
