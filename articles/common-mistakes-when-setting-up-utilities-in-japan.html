5 Mistakes Foreigners Make Setting Up Utilities in Japan (And How to Avoid Them)

Updated April 14, 2026

The first time I plugged in the kettle in my new apartment, nothing happened. No light, no sound—just silence. Turns out I’d forgotten to activate the electricity contract. That first week taught me more about setting up utilities in Japan than any guide could. Here’s what trips up most newcomers—and how to skip those headaches entirely.

The biggest mistake isn’t even about the contracts themselves. It’s assuming the process works the same way as back home. In Japan, utilities often require in-person visits, physical paperwork, and bank transfers with unique reference numbers. Even something simple like internet can turn into a two-week ordeal if you don’t know the local quirks. Here’s what I see people get wrong most often.

The first mistake is walking into the utility office without a plan. Many foreigners assume they can handle everything online or over the phone. Tokyo Electric Power Company (TEPCO) does have an English website, but their customer service reps often default to Japanese when something goes wrong. The gas company might send someone who speaks limited English. I once watched a neighbor try to set up city gas in Osaka for three weeks because the technician kept canceling appointments—the website had no English option to reschedule.

Always bring three things: your residence card (or passport if you’re on a tourist visa), your bank details, and a Japanese phone number. Without the residence card, some offices won’t even start the process. And that bank account? You’ll need it to set up automatic payments. Many utilities won’t accept cash or credit cards for the initial contract.

The second mistake is underestimating how long activation takes. Electricity and gas activation can happen immediately if you set it up during business hours. But water activation? That’s a different story. In my case, the water company required an inspection of the apartment’s plumbing before they’d turn on the service. The inspection took five days because the previous tenant had left without cleaning out the pipes. I spent those days showering at the gym and eating out—expensive lessons in patience.

Pro tip: Ask about the activation timeline before you sign anything. Some water companies in rural areas won’t activate service unless you visit their office in person. Others require you to be present when they turn it on. One friend in Fukuoka had to fly back from a trip because the water company wouldn’t activate service without her signature—despite her lease clearly stating she’d be out of town.

Another common trap is choosing the wrong internet provider for your needs. The big names like Nuro, SoftBank Air, and Docomo Hikari all advertise English support, but their actual installation processes differ wildly. Nuro, for example, requires you to be home for installation during business hours—no exceptions. SoftBank Air sends a technician with a portable router if you choose their no-installation plan, but the speeds are often half of what wired connections provide. I signed up for SoftBank Air in my first apartment because it seemed convenient. The speeds were so slow I could watch videos in 240p without buffering—something I didn’t realize until I tried streaming in 4K months later.

For wired internet, look for providers that offer “工事不要” (no construction required) options if you’re renting. Sakura Mobile and Mobal offer contracts with no upfront fees and English support, but their monthly rates are higher than local providers. One acquaintance in Yokohama switched from Sakura Mobile to Nuro after six months and cut his bill in half—while doubling his speed. Always compare the activation fees, monthly costs, and contract lengths before committing.

The third mistake is ignoring the cancellation process for previous residents’ contracts. Many newcomers assume the apartment comes with utilities already active. When I moved into my current place, the electricity was still under the previous tenant’s name. I activated my own contract, but four weeks later I got a bill for 50,000 yen in unpaid usage. The power company had simply transferred the debt to my name because the previous tenant had disappeared. I spent three months arguing with the company, submitting paperwork to prove I wasn’t responsible—only to eventually pay half the amount just to make it stop.

Always ask the landlord or real estate agent for utility history before signing a lease. If anything is in the previous tenant’s name, insist they cancel it before you move in. For electricity and gas, you can often transfer the contract to your name at the same time you set up your own service. Water contracts usually require a new application entirely, but some companies allow transfers if you provide the previous tenant’s account number.

Another trap is assuming all utilities accept foreign bank accounts. Many Japanese banks will issue you an account within weeks, but others—like Japan Post Bank—require residency status. Wise and Revolut work for most utility payments, but some companies only accept domestic transfers. One friend tried to set up internet with a Wise card only to get rejected during the credit check. He had to switch to a Japanese bank account mid-process, adding two extra weeks to his timeline.

Pro tip: Use a Japanese bank account for at least one utility even if you prefer digital options. Having a domestic account simplifies future payments and avoids issues with companies that don’t recognize international banks. The Japan Post Bank account I opened cost nothing and came with a debit card—useful for everything from utility payments to vending machines.

The fourth mistake is not understanding the difference between “基本料金” (basic fee) and usage-based charges. Many foreigners assume their bill will stay the same month to month. Electricity bills, for example, include a fixed fee plus charges based on kilowatt-hours used. In summer, my bill jumped from 5,000 yen to 12,000 yen because I ran the air conditioner constantly. Gas bills have similar structures, with higher fees in winter when heating usage spikes. Water bills are usually flat rates unless you have a water meter that measures usage precisely.

Always ask for an estimate before setting up service. TEPCO’s website has a calculator where you can input your expected usage. For gas, Tokyo Gas provides similar tools. One neighbor in Sapporo ignored these estimates and got a 30,000 yen bill in January—his first winter in the apartment. He’d assumed the fixed fee covered everything, not realizing heating costs could triple his usual bill.

The fifth mistake is overlooking the paperwork requirements for non-residents. Some utility companies require a Japanese guarantor or proof of long-term visa status. Others won’t accept applicants without a Japanese phone number. I tried to set up internet for a friend visiting from the US, only to find out his visa type disqualified him from most residential contracts. He ended up using a portable Wi-Fi device for his month-long stay.

If you’re on a short-term visa or don’t have a Japanese phone number yet, look for providers that specialize in foreign residents. Sakura Mobile and Mobal both accept foreign passport holders and offer month-to-month contracts without Japanese phone numbers. For electricity and gas, some companies like Kansai Electric Power Company have English support teams that can guide you through the process remotely.

Setting up utilities in Japan doesn’t have to be a maze. The key is preparing for the local quirks: paperwork requirements, activation timelines, and payment methods that differ from what you’re used to. Start by gathering the right documents, asking the right questions, and choosing providers that match your timeline—not just your budget. The first time I plugged in that kettle, it worked immediately. No surprises, no delays. That’s the difference knowing the common mistakes makes.
